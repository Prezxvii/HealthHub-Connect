ADHD Resource Center

Resources and support for living well with attention deficit hyperactivity disorder.

Description: ADHD is a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by persistent patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that significantly impair daily functioning.

Symptoms: Inattention (difficulty sustaining attention, making careless mistakes), hyperactivity (excessive fidgeting, talking excessively), impulsivity (acting without thinking), and difficulty organizing tasks are hallmark symptoms.

Causes: The exact cause of ADHD is unknown but likely involves a combination of genetic, neurological, and environmental factors.

Diagnosis: Diagnosis is based on clinical evaluation, including a thorough medical history, observation of behavior, and screening tools such as rating scales. Criteria outlined in the DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition) are typically used.

Treatment: Treatment approaches often include a combination of medication (stimulant medications like methylphenidate or non-stimulants like atomoxetine), behavioral therapy, and support services. Individualized treatment plans are essential.

Coping Strategies: Strategies for managing ADHD symptoms include developing routines, breaking tasks into smaller steps, using visual aids and reminders, practicing mindfulness and relaxation techniques, and seeking support from therapists, support groups, or ADHD coaches.

Hypertension (High Blood Pressure):

Description: Hypertension is a condition characterized by elevated blood pressure levels persistently above the normal range. It can lead to serious complications such as heart disease, stroke, and kidney damage.

Causes: Factors contributing to hypertension include genetics, lifestyle choices (e.g., diet, exercise), stress, and underlying health conditions.

Symptoms: Often asymptomatic, but some individuals may experience headaches, dizziness, blurred vision, and chest pain in severe cases.

Diagnosis:Blood pressure measurements taken during a physical examination help diagnose hypertension. Additional tests may be performed to assess organ damage.

Treatment: Lifestyle modifications (dietary changes, exercise, stress management) and medications (antihypertensives) are commonly used to manage hypertension.

Prevention:Adopting a healthy lifestyle, regular exercise, maintaining a balanced diet, limiting alcohol intake, and managing stress can help prevent hypertension.

Diabetes Mellitus:

Desriptions: Mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by high blood sugar levels due to insufficient insulin production or ineffective utilization of insulin.

Causes: Type 1 diabetes results from autoimmune destruction of insulin-producing cells, while type 2 diabetes often develops due to genetic and lifestyle factors such as obesity and physical inactivity.

Symptoms: Increased thirst, frequent urination, unexplained weight loss, fatigue, blurred vision, and slow wound healing are common symptoms.

Diagnosis: Blood tests measuring fasting glucose levels, oral glucose tolerance tests, and HbA1c levels help diagnose diabetes.

Treatment: Management involves lifestyle modifications (dietary changes, regular exercise), oral antidiabetic medications, insulin therapy, and monitoring blood sugar levels.

Prevention: Maintaining a healthy weight, eating a balanced diet, regular physical activity, and routine medical check-ups can reduce the risk of developing type 2 diabetes.

Asthma:

Description: Asthma is a chronic respiratory condition characterized by inflammation and narrowing of the airways, leading to recurrent episodes of wheezing, breathlessness, chest tightness, and coughing.

Causes: Genetic predisposition, environmental factors (e.g., allergens, pollutants), respiratory infections, and certain medications can trigger asthma symptoms.

Symptoms: Symptoms vary in severity and frequency, often worsening at night or early morning. They include coughing, wheezing, shortness of breath, and chest tightness.

Diagnosis: Lung function tests such as spirometry, along with medical history and physical examination, help diagnose asthma.

Treatment: Management involves avoiding triggers, using inhalers (bronchodilators, corticosteroids), and, in severe cases, oral medications or biologic therapies.

Prevention: Identifying and avoiding triggers, maintaining a clean indoor environment, staying physically active, and adhering to prescribed treatment plans can help prevent asthma attacks.

Depression:

Description: Depression is a mood disorder characterized by persistent feelings of sadness, hopelessness, and loss of interest or pleasure in activities.

Causes: Genetic factors, brain chemistry imbalance, life events (e.g., trauma, loss), medical conditions, and certain medications contribute to the development of depression.

Symptoms: Symptoms include persistent sadness, fatigue, changes in appetite or weight, sleep disturbances, feelings of worthlessness or guilt, and difficulty concentrating.

Diagnosis: Diagnosis is based on a thorough assessment of symptoms, medical history, and psychological evaluation.

Treatment: Treatment options include psychotherapy, medication (antidepressants), lifestyle modifications, and support groups.

Prevention: Maintaining a healthy lifestyle, seeking social support, managing stress effectively, and seeking professional help early can help prevent depression or reduce its severity.
